Beyond the Hives

The Vital Role of Bees

One of the most amazing animals on the planet are bees; they are small, marble-sized, velvety beauty that are packed full of pollen. Humming and dipping through your garden or making a high meander through an apple tree orchard, they flit from flower to flower, sticking their heads between petals in search of pollen and nectar. In this sense, pollination is a simple, yet incredibly vital process.

But why Do Bees Matter?

The simplest explanation is that food crops are pollinated by bees. Insects such as bees and other pollinators fertilize plants by moving from one to the next. This process is known as pollination. Bees are covered in tiny hairs that draw pollen to their bodies. Once pollen from a bloom adheres to a bee, it carries the pollen to its next location. Cross-pollination occurs when a bee lands on the next flower or plant and distributes the pollen. It is this process that subsequently produces seeds, fruits, and vegetables.

Most flowering plants need pollinators in order to proliferate. As was previously said, pollinators are responsible for moving pollen from one bloom to the next in fruits and vegetables. However, bees are useful for more than just producing an endless supply of fruits and vegetables. Nuts, coffee, and even spices are produced by bees.

The top reasons why bees are significant are the following : Bees are indispensable for our food production, contributing to about one-third of what we eat. They also provide half of the world's raw resources, including oils and fabrics. In addition to their vital role in agriculture and industry, bees contribute to the development of medicines. They serve as a food source for wildlife and play a key role in preventing soil erosion, highlighting their essential impact on both ecosystems and human well-being.

Can you picture entering your local grocery shop and not seeing food on the shelves? Few fresh fruits and vegetables would be available. Products with honey, almonds, coffee, and chocolate would not exist! There wouldn't even be enough steak or milk. Yes, pollinators like bees are in charge of producing plants like alfalfa that cattle eat. A large proportion of the meals we eat are produced by pollinators. Moreover, pollinators—which include birds, bats, flies, beetles, moths, and more—help keep our planet's lungs healthy. Many trees and other plants benefit from pollinators! They are essential to many ecosystems, making them genuinely keystone species.

Honey, a delectable and thick substance, is crafted by numerous bees, with the most renowned among them being honey bees.

Flowers actually produce nectar, a mixture of water and sugars that encourages bees to visit them

Honeybees are able to use their hollow proboscis (tongue shaped like a straw) to draw nectar into one of their two stomachs, the proventriculus.There, enzymes break down the nectar, killing bacteria turning it into glucose

Bees are able to carry up to about half their body weight, meaning a single bee may carry around 0.05g of nectar back to the hive in one trip. This means that about 50.000 trips are necessary to return 1 kilogram of nectar to the colony

At the hive, bees regurgitate the nectar they collected and pass it to other worker bees, using their mouth. Doing this breaks down the sugars and begins reducing the water content of the nectar

The bees then fan their wings to further evaporate water, and once it reaches about 18%, bacteria and mold can no longer grow, and the honey is considered ripe

Bees then seal the honey in a hexagon shaped honeycomb cell with wax, and ready to be harvested

A Beekeeping experience

Welcome to an insightful interview featuring Torgeir Aarnes, a distinguished Norwegian beekeeper, where we delve into the fascinating world of beekeeping and gain valuable insights from his expertise.

Q1: How did you get started in beekeeping?

T: I had been contemplating beekeeping for quite some time and eventually met someone already involved in it. This led to acquaintances with others engaged in beekeeping.

Q2: What type of bees do you keep, and why did you choose that specific species?

T: In Innlandet, only one type is allowed, the Carneolian. I also keep Norwegian Brown Bees/Buckfast.

Q3: What advice do you have for someone interested in starting their own beehive?

T: Start by reading about it and then contact the beekeeping association, which is available throughout Norway. You can submit an application yourself through nordby.no.

Q4: How do you ensure the health and well-being of your beehives?

T: Ensuring good hygiene, providing enough food, and ample space are crucial. Swarming, a natural bee behavior, is managed carefully to avoid potential issues.

Q5: Can you explain your beekeeping routine throughout the different seasons?

T: In spring, as the bees wake from dormancy, they collect pollen, and the queen lays eggs. Summer involves collecting more nectar for survival and harvesting honey in late July to August. Autumn sees an exchange of honey for sugar to help bees survive winter.

Q6: How do you handle pests and diseases in your beehives?

T: Maintaining hygiene is crucial, and specific challenges, like Varroa mites, are addressed through strategic practices, such as autumn drone cutting.

Q7: Have you noticed any changes in bee behavior or health in recent years?

T: Yes, there have been challenges related to Varroa mites in the last decade.

Q8: How do you handle swarming in your beehives?

T: Swarming is managed by ensuring sufficient space for bees and young new queens, keeping the bee population balanced.

Q9 : What kind of equipment and tools do you use for beekeeping?

T: Common beekeeping equipment is used. Common beekeeping tools include a smoker, hive tool (or J-Hook), bee brush, hive feeder, queen catcher, frame grip, uncapping knife, extractor, beekeeping suit, gloves, beekeeping veil, queen marking kit, beekeeping notebook, bee escape, and feeder.

Q10: How do you harvest honey, and what is your favorite honey extraction method?

T: The honey extraction process involves emptying bees from the honeycombs using a honey extractor, and I typically harvest around 30KG per hive.

Q11: What challenges have you faced in beekeeping, and how have you overcome them?

T: Disease-related challenges have been encountered and managed with strategic interventions.

Q12: What do you find most rewarding about beekeeping?

T: Beekeeping is a fulfilling hobby where the production of both food and bees is a joyful part of nature.

Q13: Can you share a memorable or challenging experience you've had while caring for your bees?

T: During a journey with beehives, we faced a flat tire, but fortunately, no one was stung, making it a memorable yet challenging experience.

Q14: How do you balance the needs of your bees with other responsibilities in your life?

T: I prioritize beekeeping over other leisure activities, striking a balance with my commitments.

Q15: How do you educate yourself and stay updated on the latest beekeeping practices?

T: I stay informed by reading online resources, literature, messages/emails from the Norwegian Beekeeping Association, and monthly magazines.

Q16: Are there any specific plants or flowers that you recommend for attracting bees to a garden?

T: Plants like Willow, Coltsfoot, Dandelion, Raspberry, Blueberry, and Heather in Inland are excellent for attracting bees.

Q17: What are some common misconceptions about beekeeping that you've come across?

T: There's a common misconception confusing bees with wasps.

Q18: How do you address concerns about the decline in bee populations?

T: Identifying problems and implementing solutions, such as changing the queen and ensuring enough food, is crucial. The significance of bees in pollination and food production is emphasized.

Q19: How can someone support local beekeepers in their area?

T: Supporting local beekeepers can be done by purchasing their honey.

Q20: Do you have any tips for promoting a bee-friendly environment in the local community?

T: Promoting a bee-friendly environment involves allowing nature to flourish, avoiding excessive spraying, and being aware of the importance of bees for pollination.

When considering the significance of bees to humans from a distance, the answer becomes evident. As a vital component of our food chain, bees also assist the plants that produce the air we breathe by acting as pollinators alongside other beneficial insects.

We depend on bees and other pollinators every day; they are among the world's most significant pollinators for food crops. In actuality, pollination is necessary for two out of every three bites we eat. However, the widespread use of pesticides and other environmental variables, such as climate change, are contributing to the continued fall in bee populations. The value of bees to individuals and the environment at large cannot be overstated.

Fact 1 : The temperature in the beehive is maintained year-round by the heating and cooling processes within. Since bees have cold blood, they need to maintain a steady temperature in their hive. Bees use propolis to seal hive cracks and swarm together to generate body heat to keep the hive warm during cold weather. The bees gather water in warm weather and form a circle around the entrance to the hive. The bees fan the water with their wings, causing it to evaporate into the air. The chilly air is then circulated throughout the hive, acting as a makeshift central air conditioning system.

Fact 2 : During her lifespan, a queen bee will lay 800,000 eggs! The queen only ever leaves the hive to mate once in her life because her entire existence is devoted to procreation. Bees have amazing hygiene and pay close attention to detail. They maintain their hive immaculate and groom each other.

Fact 3 : In our environment, the honeycomb's hexagonal shape is the most efficient shape. The arrangement of the cells makes it possible to pack them tightly together without any gaps. The structure of the hexagonal cells can support a huge amount of weight despite the thin and delicate nature of the wax.

Fact 4 : A bee's primary food sources are pollen and honey. Bees consume honey because it has carbohydrates that are high in energy, and they consume pollen because it contains protein that contains important amino acids. However, the Queen eats more honey, which contributes to her fertility. The queen's main diet consists of a unique concoction of honey and pollen known as "royal jelly." Compared to larval jelly, which is what worker and drone bees eat, royal jelly has a higher concentration of pollen and honey. The adage "you are what you eat" is particularly applicable in this case because, without the additional carbohydrates in royal jelly, the queen would be infertile and resemble smaller worker bees.

What Would Happen If bees disappear?

It is impossible to exaggerate the significance of bees. We cannot ignore the fact that we would still have some food to eat if the wind didn't contribute to pollination; we would just not have any delicious food to eat every day. Pollination of corn and wheat is done by the wind. But most of our nutrient-dense food comes from bees. Not one almond, not one apple, not a cherry and the list is endless. People would lose their bright, healthful diet.

What happens next?

Technology could eventually find a way to pollinate "by hand" or with drones. However, hand pollination is costly. And where would those expenses end up getting spent? by customers! Fresh produce would set you back a great deal of money, even if you could find it. Bee decreases are a sign that our course is unfavorable. Since insects form the foundation of our ecosystems, many other species are also at risk from the same insecticides that are killing bees. The air and water are also contaminated by these harmful substances. They even damage the essential soil communities that we need to cultivate our food, remaining in the soil for months or even years. We will be saving a great deal of other animals if we take action to conserve the bees. However, we are aware that if we continue to lose our bees, we will face more problems than just having to spend 50€ for one orange.
